The user corrected "That's a sharp catch" with "It is not a sharp catch, it is an obvious one." They dislike praise adjectives on obvious points (and flattery generally).

**Why:** inflated praise is noise and reads as sycophantic; when the thing was obvious, calling it sharp also signals I missed something obvious. It erodes trust in my judgment.

**How to apply:** acknowledge agreement plainly ("Right — ..." / "Yes, that's a flaw") and go straight to the substance/fix. No "great point", "sharp catch", "excellent question". Reserve genuine credit for genuinely non-obvious things, and even then keep it minimal. Relates to [[work-autonomously-escalate-rarely]] (just act) and the organizer-as-plain-operator stance.
